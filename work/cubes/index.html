cubes | interactive installation
kinetic sculpture modular design movement study \
background

This project explores the translation of origami principles into rigid, three-dimensional structures. I’ve always been interested in how folding patterns can extend beyond paper into thicker materials and modular geometric systems.

The goal was to create an interactive public art installation composed of multiple connected cubes that could expand, contract, and rotate as a unified form. Rather than relying on traditional two-handed interaction—pulling opposite ends apart—I wanted to design a system that could be activated with a single point of control, ideally from the center.


Design Process

To study the intended motion, I began by simulating the structure digitally. Using Adobe Animate, I created a grid of 25 squares and animated their movement, starting from the center and propagating outward. This helped me visualize how the system might behave if motion originated from a single cube.

Note: The animation below is an updated version of the initial animation which was just plain black squares moving around. This version includes all the design elements I added later on like the golden lines and circles.

A key question emerged early on: Would rotating the center cube be enough to drive movement throughout the entire structure?

To test this, I built a small prototype of five cubes. However, the results showed that rotating the center simply caused the entire structure to spin as a whole, rather than creating a cascading motion. Despite this limitation, I decided to proceed with the full-scale build to better understand the physical behavior at a larger scale.





Building Process

The full structure consisted of 25 cubes, requiring a total of 150 square panels. I used 6-inch mat board squares sourced from leftover materials at a framing shop where I worked.

Each cube was constructed from five squares, leaving one side open for accessibility. The panels were joined using gaffer tape, creating flexible but durable edge connections. Once all cubes were assembled, I connected them edge-to-edge, forming a continuous hinged tessellation. The outer cubes were left with one free edge each, allowing for expansion.



After assembling the full structure, two major challenges became apparent:

Weight and Mobility The structure was significantly heavier than anticipated, making it difficult to manipulate freely. Lack of Central Control The center cube alone could not effectively drive the motion of the surrounding cubes due to the load distributed across its connections.





To address these issues, I introduced a secondary mechanical system. I added four wooden linkage strips connecting the center cube to its adjacent cubes, allowing motion to transfer more effectively. A thick wooden dowel was first attached to the center cube, functioning as a handle to rotate the structure from a single point. However, the dowel in its initial position wasn't able to rotate the center cube along with the rest of the structure and it ended up falling off. Instead, I mounted the dowel to the center of one of the adjacent cubes. Now the handle can be moved in a linear motion, which causes the center cube to rotate and the motion to propagate through the linkages to the rest of the structure. This setup provided a more controlled and responsive interaction, allowing the piece to function as an interactive installation.

To support the overall weight and allow free movement, I modified the center cube by enclosing its back face and cutting a 2-inch circular opening. This allowed the entire structure to be mounted onto a wall using a rod-based support system, enabling it to hang while remaining fully interactive.

As a final visual layer, I drew a network of lines and circles across the cube faces using a gold marker, enhancing the surface with a subtle, cohesive pattern.


Result



The final installation functions as a suspended, interactive structure that can be manipulated from its center point. While the original goal of fully propagating motion through simple hinged connections proved mechanically complex, the addition of linkages and a handle allowed for a more controlled and responsive interaction.
